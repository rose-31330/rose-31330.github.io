Earlier this year I took part in the CubeSat Concurrent Engineering Workshop organised by ESA, and I came away with a much clearer sense of how space missions actually get built. Tt turned out to be messier, faster, and more collaborative than anyone had suggested.

The premise was straightforward and really interesting: design a CubeSat mission from scratch in four days, built around a rideshare opportunity with EnVision, ESA's upcoming Venus orbiter. The CubeSat would deploy ahead of EnVision's own science operations, carrying out its own measurements before the main mission begins. Scientifically interesting, logistically complicated, and, apparently, achievable in a week if you move fast enough.

I was part of the trajectory and mission analysis team. While half of our team ran full GMAT simulations, our job was to produce preliminary delta-v budgets (the fuel costs of orbital insertion and end-of-life de-orbit) quickly enough that the propulsion, thermal, and payload teams could actually use them. It felt less like engineering and more like a relay race: rough calculations out of MATLAB, handed off immediately, refined as constraints (and complaints) came back from other sub-teams. You learn very quickly that spacecraft design is less about finding the optimal solution and more about finding a solution everyone can live with.

We also got to visit ESA's CubeSat Support Facility and ESEC Redu, which was a reminder that behind all the fun we were having there is actual hardware, infrastructure, and people who have spent careers building it. That was probably the best part: meeting engineers and students from across Europe and Canada, comparing notes on our own CubeSat projects, and realising how much of this industry runs on exactly that kind of informal exchange.
